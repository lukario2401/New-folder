asthma sympthoms and how to cure it

Bronchial asthma is a long-lasting inflammation of the respiratory system. It leads to recurring episodes of wheezing, chest tightness, coughing, and trouble breathing. These symptoms often happen at night or in the early morning. The inflammation and narrowing of the airways cause these symptoms. Genetics and environmental factors are the main causes of asthma development and worsening. People with bronchial asthma may have the following signs and symptoms: Difficulty breathing Feeling of tightness in the chest Wheezing Continuous coughing that disturbs sleep For patients over 5-6 years old, lung function is evaluated with tests like spirometry or Peak Expiratory Flow to assess how air moves in and out of the lungs and identify any airflow changes.After diagnosis, a healthcare provider will prescribe asthma medication, such as inhalers and pills. They may also recommend lifestyle changes to manage and prevent future asthma attacks. Although asthma cannot be cured, there are effective medications available to prevent symptoms.

Nose

When you breathe in, air flows through your nostrils and enters the nasal cavity. Cells in the nasal cavity release mucus. This mucus contains enzymes called lysozymes. They kill bacteria for us. The outside of the nasal cavity has nose hairs that get coated with the mucus. This helps them trap a lot of different slightly harmful particles, etc.

Larynx

The larynx is a tube that allows air to travel from your throat to your lungs. It contains your vocal cords as well! Some people call it the voice box.

The trachea is a tube that connects the larynx to the bronchi! It's main function is to transport air. When we inhale, the air goes through a long(or not very) journey that includes going through the trachea to get to the lungs.

Normal

Normal lungs and asthma lungs have several key differences. Asthma lungs are characterized by chronic inflammation of the airways, resulting in swollen and narrow passages. In asthma lungs, inflammation leads to hypersensitivity and hyperreactivity of the airway smooth muscles, resulting in stronger constriction and difficulty in breathing.Asthma lungs also produce excessive mucus, which can form plugs and further obstruct the airways. In terms of alveolar function, asthma can impair gas exchange, reducing oxygen intake and increasing carbon dioxide retention.
